From Malthus Model to Logistic Model

Mathematical derivation

Malthus population model tells us that the growth rate is propotional to the population with a constant coefficient. Mathematical Speaking, this can be described by using differential equation:

dpdt=rp

where r is the constant growth rate.

Actually, due to the limitation of sources and environment spaces, r will decrease as p Increases.

The simplies way to model this relationship is to assume they are linked linearly, r(p)=a+bp, with initial conditions

  • r(0)=r
  • r(K)=0

The first condition indicates a internal grow trend for a certain population while the second condition expresses the idea that when the population reachs to a certain threshold K, population will stop growing.

These two initial conditions makes it possible to solve for the a and b

  • a=r
  • b=−rK

Therefore:

r(p)=r−rKp

we can use the new r to replace the one in Malthus model and finally get the logistic model:

dpdt=rp(1−pK)

Moreover, evaluate this differential equation, we can get a beautiful ''S'' shape function:

p(t)=p(0)Kexp(rt)K+p(0)(exp(rt)−1)

 

Haverst model

Imaging that we want to model the fish population in a sea and the fish is the main source for fisher man's suvival. In this system, we assume that the fish will grow logistically and the population of them will decrease only due to the effort of fishing. Then we can propose a Haverst model:

dpdt=rp(1−pK)−h

where

h={Ep   0≤p≤Y0/EY0   Y0/E≤p

This indicates that the effect of fishing will grow proportional to the fish population and then remain constant, Y0 is the maximum yield for fishermen.

Further

As above, to describe the input effect of a system, apart from just a constant rate assumption, logistic model seems much better since the beautiful ''s'' shape function indicates a common principle that the element of a certain system grows very slowy at the very begining, then grows exponetionally and finally slow down like the logarithmic function. Moreover, it can also be inserted to ODE systems like competition model and Prey-Predator model to describe the iteraction of two species.
