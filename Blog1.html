How my mathematical interests evolved?

I am about to start my final year as an undergraduate so this is a brief reminiscence of my mathematics study for previous year.

Thomas Malthus

I am so lucky that I still have huge interests in mathematics after two years study and I also find which area should I devote myserlf to do in the future. I could still remember that when I was in first year, Dr. Jie Yang taught us Applied mathematics, I was captivated by the Malthus population model in her lecture, the basic background is that the end of the 18th century, before the Britain industrial revolution, a great thinkers called Malthus proposed a daunting problem: human population grows exponentially while the amount of food grows algebraically, so when the growth of population is more than food, human will inevitably end up to starvation, disease and even war.

 

"Through the animal and vegetable kingdoms, nature has scattered the seeds of life abroad with the most profuse and liberal hand. ... The germs of existence contained in this spot of earth, with ample food, and ample room to expand in, would fill millions of worlds in the course of a few thousand years. Necessity, that imperious all pervading law of nature, restrains them within the prescribed bounds. The race of plants, and the race of animals shrink under this great restrictive law. And the race of man cannot, by any efforts of reason, escape from it. Among plants and animals its effects are waste of seed, sickness, and premature death. Among mankind, misery and vice. "

— Thomas Malthus, 1798. An Essay on the Principle of Population. Chapter I. [1]

 

However, this kind of model could be suitable for explanation of some historical facts in acient civilization but it does not make sense for modern life due to the development of technology after the industrial revolution, from an overview of the human, the population is growing while our living standard is also been improved. But what Malthus described as the "Ending of human" is actually the prototype of fix point of equilibrium point. So here comes the Logistics model, which could be seen as an improvement of Malthus model, it also gives us a way to analyze scientific problems from a qualitative viewm, that is, phase line for qualitative analysis of the behavior of a system. Logistics model could be built by simply adding an additional assumption that the net birth rate r=b−d could not be a constant, but decreases as the population increases instead, the simpliest way to redefine the r is to use linear relationship, r=ap+b. If we proposed initial conditions, r(0)=r, r(K)=0, we could solve the parameters, a=−Kr,b=r, namely, r(p)=r−rKp, replacing the r in Malthus model we could get the Logistics model. Logistics model used a more explicit way to demonstrate the destiny of system, imaging that a innovative idea is proposed by a company and the company want explore new market for this idea, at the very beginning, it is very difficult to see any change since new industry faces lots of challenges. As time goes, a lot of research and development are done, this kind of industry grows exponentially and the company makes large amount of money because of this; Later, attracted by the huge profits of this industry, competitors start to appear in this market, and the pace of making profits slows significantly, like the logarithmic function, approaching a certain threshold gradually.

 

At that time, I also learned the Lotka-Volterra predator-prey model and Competition Models, which includes the interaction between species, by reading the book "Advanced Engineering Mathematics" by Erwin Kreyszig. I gradually knew how powerful was the differential equations and I also realized that it is of great importance to learn more skills of applied mathematics, such as complex analysis, fourier analysis and vector calculus.

 

Covid-19

Then, the outbreak of the Covid-19 made me stuck in my hometown, a small countryside in Sichuan. Thanks to the internet, which makes it possible for me to get the latest information around the world, such as the huge contribution of The Johns Hopkins University for their website data about the Covid-19 cases report, leading work done by Neil Ferguson from Imperial College London and his teams, which was helpful for the UK government policy-making, lectures carried by Dr. Nanshan Zhong for sharing the transmission route, infectivity, diagnosis and management of Covid-19 infection and large amount of mathematical models about the prediction and analysis about the Covid-19, etc.

 

Further, I'd like to say a special thank you to my uncle, who is an excellent surgeon and at that special time , he had to come to his hospital daily to help fighting with the virus. After his daily work and came back to our village, we always had a nice conversation about the Covid-19, he told me that how his hospital did the epidemiological investigation for patients, epidemiological characteristics of the Covid-19 patients, how to use traditional Chinese medicine to cure patients and the special management of a hopital during pandemic and so on, while I would codified the articles I searched from the Internet in the day time, like The New England Journal of Medicine, The Lancet etc, and shared with him, he also taught me how to use the Radiographs of Chest to make a judgement for people since the lung will change pathologically after the attack of the virus.

 

As more and more academic research came up around the world, my previous sense of incompetence about my own inability to fight with Covid-19 disappears, this was due to the fact that mathematicians played an important role in this fighting, even though they looks out of this discipline. Mathematical modelling and computational simulation made the prediction and evolvement of the virus controllable. Famous models like SIR, SEIR, SLIAR, SEQIJR and so on are all based on the differential equations to describe the behaviors of different kind of population, moreover, statistical skills like parameter estimation made the model as accurate as possible. For example, a mathematical term, R0, shows how contagious an infectious disease is. It tells us the average number of people who contract an infectious disease from people who have this disease, I was sure that how this mathematical term meant to the government and their people since when I was later in the UK, R0 is a very good indicator for me to know the current situation of where I was, and for government to modify their current and future work on policy-making. Personally, I tried to use python to do the computational simulation based on SIR model, although it was just a simple try, it made me realize the importance of making mathematical model numerical and visualized by the perfomance of computer.

 

Dynamical system and computational mathematics

For the second year study, I went to the UK, which was a great chance for me to explore the mathematics. Dr. Rachel Nicks and Dr. Etienne Farcot taught us Modelling with differential equations, I learned how to use phase line and phase plane to analyze the behavior of a system, which was a nice extension for my interests. "Nonlinear dynamics and chaos'' by Steven H. Strogatz brought me to the nonlinear beauty of this world, it also gave me a brief knowledge of this area, such as the history of nonlinear dynamics, qualitative theorems like phase plane analysis, limit cycles and their bifurcations, chaos, and even fractals. In order to enhance my knowledge of this area, I used ''Differential Equations, Dynamical Systems, and an Introduction to Chaos'' by Morris et al. to teach myself about 2D ODE systems at the end of 2020. I've gained a basic roadmap of learning dynamical system for my further study because of this book and it is also this book that make me to choose course Mathematical Biology and Medicine for my final year study due to the Hodgkin-Huxley model mentioned in this book.

Based on my interest in dynamical systems, I got a chance to do a summer research about it under the supervision of Dr. Martin Richter, which was funded by the School of Mathematical Sciences, The University of Nottingham. I derived a formula for the electronic transport in a so called two-dimensional electron gas (2DEG) and solved the equations of motion in presence of a magnetic field and then defined a boudary map in Birkhoff coordinates, which is also called Poincaré Section. Throughout this process, computational mathematics skills I learned from the course Introduction to Scientific Computation played an very important role, especially the Bifurcation I found after the visualization of the Poincaré Section under different magnetic field values. Moreover, transfer operator was created to allow for the propagation of densities of initial conditions, which was also consistent with the experiment data. In short, this experience is a good application of dynamical system and computational mathematics, solving the problems in both qualitative and numerical way.
