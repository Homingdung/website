Some Models in Newton Mechanics

Newton's law (1687)

Newton, an excellent British scientist, formulated the three known laws:

  • N1: Unless a force acts on the particle, the particle will remain stationary (or more generally, moving at a constant speed).
∑F=0⟺dvdt=0

 

  • N2: The resultant force acting on a particle is equal to its rate of change of momentum.
F=dpdt=d(mv)dt=mdvdt=ma

 

  • N3: When two particles apply force to each other, they are equal in magnitude, in opposite direction, and act along the line connecting the particles (or, for an equilibrium system, for each action, there is an equal and opposite response).
f1=−f2

Examples of this blog will use the below common forces:

  • f=0: equilibrium
  • f=mg:uniform gravitational acceleration
  • f=−mkv=−mkx˙ (k>0): drag force
  • f=−mω2x: restoring force

Models

Restoring force

A particle of unit mass moves in 1D, given that x(t)=cos(t), where x is the displacement from the origin.

By Newton second law, we can find the equation of motion.

f=x¨=−cos(t)=−x

Thus, the force is actually proportional to the displacement of the particle from the origin and is in the opposite direction. Restoring means it will pull the particle back towards the origin.

 

Drag force

Considering a particle of mass m moves in 1D subject to a force f=−mkx˙.

Model this motion if the particle starts at the origin, with speed V moving to the right.

It is convenient to denote v=x˙, we can get the equation of the motion by Newton second law:

mv˙=−mkv

Solve this ODE with the initial condition v(0)=V we will get:

v(t)=Vexp(−kt)

Intergration could give us x(t) with the initial condition x(0)=0:

x(t)=Vk(1−exp(−kt))

Moreover, we could find that if t→∞, x→V/k. Mathematically speaking, the force acts proportionally to the velocity in the opposite direction. In reality, the force is a classical model of air resistance when a particle is realsed from a certain height.

 

Damping (Damped Simple Harmonic Motion)

Simple harmonic motion is the equation of motion satisfying:

x¨+ω2x=0

And the general solution is:

x=Acos(ωt)+Bsin(ωt)

where A and B are constants.

We know that simple harmonic motion will finally come to rest dut the effect of damping force or drag force. Like the draging force, we use a simple damping force, which is proportional to the velocity:

Fdamp=−kx˙

for constant k>0

 

The model is that:

x¨+px˙+ω2x=0,with p,ω≥0

If we use μ1, μ2 to represent the roots of −p±p2−4ω22

  • Case1: p2−4ω2 positive, heavy damping, exponetial decay
  • Case2: p2−4ω2 negative, light damping, exponetial decay of oscillations
  • Case3: p2−4ω2 zero, critical damping, no sustained oscillations

Forcing

If we add an external force, which is time-dependent, the model will be:

x¨+px˙+ω2x=f(t),where f(t)=F(t)m

Considering a periodic forcing, f(t)=bcos(Ωt)

x¨+px˙+ω2x=bcos(Ωt)

Since this ODE is inhomogeneous, the solution will be

x(t)=particular integral+complementary function

The complementary function is the one of the previous homogenous ODE while the particular integral is the expression of the form:

xp=λcos(Ωt)+νsin(Ωt)

where

λ=(ω2−Ω2)b(ω2−Ω2)2+p2Ω2,ν=pΩb(ω2−Ω2)2+p2Ω2
xp(t)=bcos(Ωt−Φ)((ω2−Ω2)2+p2Ω2)1/2,where tan(Φ)=pΩω2−Ω2

Thus, the general solution is:

x(t)=Aexp(−p/2+p2−4ω2/2)t+Bexp(−p/2−p2−4ω2/2)t+bcos(Ωt−Φ)((ω2−Ω2)2+p2Ω2)1/2

The first two terms are called transient part since it will tend to 0 as t→∞. The last term is called persistent part since it is kind of steady-state for oscillarting.
